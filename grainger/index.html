01

Consolidate in China

Recall that 89% of cargo ships factory-direct with 300+ suppliers each loading their own containers, leaning on small, inefficient 20′ boxes. Should Grainger open a consolidation center at the port of Yantian and re-pack that volume into full 40′ containers. Does the freight saving cover the cost of running the center?

A 20′ container holds about half the cargo of a 40′ box but costs roughly 80% as much. Yantian moves ~62,700 cbm a year across a mix of both.

Grainger needs to consider three additional costs against the freight saving: (i) inventory dwelling at the consolidation center, (ii) a per-cbm material-handling charge to re-pack, and (iii) an annual fixed cost of running the operation. Drag the sliders to obtain the total savings from consolidation in different scenarios.

02

Open a West-Coast import DC

A single national DC in Kansas City pools demand beautifully — but it forces West-Coast cargo to rail 1,570 miles inland, then truck back out. A new import DC at the port (the WCDC) can receive that cargo and serve the West directly. The catch: split the network and you lose pooling, so safety stock and handling rise. Which DCs should it serve?

Assigning a DC to the WCDC and its cargo skips the inland round-trip, which is a transport win for Grainger. But every DC assigned to the WCDC, reduces the inventory pooled in Kansas City increasing; the second stocking point (WCDC) also needs its own safety stock.

Try Los Angeles alone first as the DC assigned to WCDC. Then add Dallas (the ROI falls). Why? Dallas is cheap to reach from Kansas City but expensive to truck from the coast, so the savings reduce.

The key tradeoff is that lower inland transport cost requires more decentralization; lower inventory and handling costs require more centralization.

03

Why the two moves belong together

Neither decision is only about the dollars on this page. Together they reposition Grainger for the growth that actually motivated the project.

Complement A

Pack once, for the right door

Consolidation centers can build containers labeled per destination — Kansas City vs. the West Coast — so the WCDC barely needs to de-consolidate. Decision 01 lowers the handling penalty in Decision 02.

Complement B

Move work upstream

Once cargo is consolidated in China, kitting and quality checks can shift to low-cost ports instead of Kansas City — savings this single-port model never captures.

Complement C

Reach new markets directly

The same China centers can ship straight to Mexico, Canada, Asia and Latin America — the international growth that the Kansas-City-first network made prohibitively expensive.
